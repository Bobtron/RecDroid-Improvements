Comment #1

Posted on Feb 2, 2013 by Quick Hippo

So if you can connect with "check certificate" disabled and can't with "check certificate" enabled, it's the proof that the option works !

The check certificate will NOT authorize a certificate considered as invalid (for example self signed -- probably your case ! --, not matching domain etc etc etc).

Maybe some example can make it things more clear : when on your web browser you go on a https server that have a self signed certificate (not trusted by known CA). On web browser you'll get an error about the fact the certificate seems not trust-able as not signed by a trusted authority (not in CA list). Depending on the browser it's definitely impossible to reach the website, or you'll have to temporarily ignore this certificate error (or to definitely trust the certificate in firefox and add it to local certificates).

In CSipSimple, in such a case, there is only two possible case : - "check certificate" option enabled => If there is a certificate error : DON'T trust it - "check certificate" option disabled => Consider the certificate you got is the good one and that there is no MitM injecting a certifcate not trusted by a trusted 3rd party (the CA list).

So if I correctly understand your report, the behavior you observe is NOT a bug and is the expected behavior.

Errors reported by certificate are basically done by openssl library. And then pjsip library (that is also widely used and tested on other platforms) decide to trust or not the certificate depending on your "check certificate" option. You can find more details on each of these expert options here : http://www.pjsip.org/docs/latest/pjsip/docs/html/structpjsip__tls__setting.htm (check certificate correspond to the "pjsip_tls_setting::verify_client"

I advise you to learn more about how certificates works. It's not obvious, but understanding how it works is very important. Those that trusts certificates not signed by 3rd party authority certificates should be aware of what does that means. Trusting a certificate not checked by a 3rd party or not coherent with domain names can still keep some level of security as the exchanges will remain encrypted, but it's not safe to MitM attacks when the certificate is retrieved (and this also apply to https and web browsers).
